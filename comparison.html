GALLIPOLI AND PASSCHENDAELE:

WHICH ONE WAS WORSE?

New Zealand’s participation in the First World War initially occurred in Gallipoli in 1915, in which the nation lost many of its soldiers’ lives to bloodshed. Fast-forward to two years later in 1917, in the battle of Passchendaele, the country once again experienced the horrors of warfare, which is now most commonly known as New Zealand’s worst military disaster as of yet. However, what happened in Passchendaele is often neglected and overshadowed by what had occurred in Gallipoli, causing for most people in today’s generation to be misinformed or not even informed at all on what the New Zealand soldiers had done and sacrificed for the nation.

In this page, you can read about what had happened in both events.

GALLIPOLI

During World War I, the Battle of Gallipoli, which occurred from 19th of February, 1915 to 9th of January, 1916, was known for its failed effort in controlling the sea route from Europe to Russia. Due to countless of propaganda posters persuading them to join the army, numerous Kiwi men had blindingly volunteered and participated to be a part of the Australian and New Zealand Army Corps (ANZAC), clueless of what was about to happen. Due to lack of training and knowledge of the land in which they were battling in, 7500 innocent Kiwi soldiers have died during the battle. Because of this, Gallipoli continues to remain as a bitter and horrifying memory for both families of the affected NZ soldiers and New Zealanders today.

PASSCHENDAELE

From 31st of July 1917 to 10th of November 1917, the battle of Passchendaele also occurred during the first World War to fight against the German Empire. It was also considered as a fruitless campaign as within just one single day, now known as ‘New Zealand’s Blackest Day’, a total of 800 soldiers and 45 officers were killed along with 2700 wounded during the 12th of October 1917. Before this, our country had already lost 320 New Zealanders. In total, since 12th of October, 1917, New Zealand had experienced 3,865 casualties in total. This is considered a lot for a considerably short period of time.

While it may seem that the battle of Gallipoli is considered to be more recognised by most New Zealanders today than the battle in Passchendaele, both tragedies have helped to mould and further understand our identity as a country. Both wars have also caused immense pain for the relatives of those who were affected and have died.
