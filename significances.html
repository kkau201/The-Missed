SIGNIFICANCE

Since the aftermath of the war, the Battle of Passchendaele gradually gained significance in the hearts of New Zealanders in the following years. Due to being known as not only one of the greatest military disasters in New Zealand history, it had also affected individuals around the globe.

The nation continues to commemorate the great soldiers and other affected New Zealanders during that time. An example of this was on October 12, 2012, where a national commemorative service was held at the National War Memorial in Auckland and Tomb of the Unknown Warrior in Wellington to celebrate the 95th Anniversary of the Battle of Passchendaele.

Across the globe, many New Zealanders come and visit Passchendaele memorials in Belgium every year, which not only help show the country’s pride towards our soldiers but also help in the exchanging of cultures and forming a unity between both countries. Such places were found at Gravenstafel in Ypres, Belgium, a place where New Zealanders were attacked and killed on October 4, 1917. A monument of a Maori carving with a badge with an engraving of a fern leaf and ‘New Zealand’ over crossed tiaha is found. Situated between Broodseinde and Passchendaele, a cemetery known as the Tyne Cot is also found, of which 520 are of New Zealand headstones. Out of this, 322 headstones have ‘Known Unto God’ engraved on it due to the fact that their names were unknown.

1,179 unnamed killed New Zealanders at Passchendaele are named at the ‘Missing’ memorial, which they were described as ‘Here are recorded names of the officers and men of New Zealand who fell in the Battle of Broodseinde and the first battle of Passchendaele, October 1917, and whose graves are known only to God’ (Naumann, R., 2009).

Along with this, numerous projects, such as the Passchendaele Project, have been established to pay homage to the NZ soldiers who have sacrificed their lives since October 12, 1917, through collating their past personal photos and letters from their relatives. The New Zealand Ministry of Culture and Heritage have also released a website which consisted of an archival film, photos, stories and history based on Passchendaele.

The younger generations, especially students of today’s society, are also becoming more aware of the history behind Passchendaele as it is now commonly taught as a topic in schools. Additionally, more books have been published on its history since the tragedy. Because of this, many students have been inspired to create poems, essays and songs on the battle of Passchendaele.
